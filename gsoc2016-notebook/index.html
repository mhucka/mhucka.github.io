Notebook usage notes
Notes in a notebook are in chronological order as much as possible.
Level 1 rows are dates. This way, in OmniOutliner, it’s convenient to be able to see all the dates in the left-hand content pane.
I use headings to indicate changes in topics or lines of thought
The heading style is “Heading 1”.
Notes under a heading often involve the same topic — whatever I happen to be working on or thinking about. When I change directions, I try to introduce a heading to indicate the change. This makes reading notes later.
Handling clippings
PDFs, clippings and other pasted-in material must be inserted in the “Clippings” column for a given row.
Use the notes to indicate the source, or provide a URL.
Even though pasted-in material should have a note that indicates the URL, the items themselves should be linked to their sources.
Flag column
The flag column has a few symbols that can be used to annotate notes. This is for reminders to myself to pay extra attention to something, or to job my memory.

What is the data type of rdf:ID in Biopax documents?
The type is listed as RDF/XML ID. Looking around for a definition, after some effort, I finally traced things down to the page at http://www.w3.org/TR/REC-rdf-syntax/#rdf-id wherein it states that it is "An attribute ·string-value· matching any legal [XML-NS] token NCName". Now looking up the XML data type NCName, we find it in http://www.w3.org/TR/REC-xml-names/#NT-NCName that it states
An XML Name, minus the ":"
That's then defined in terms of something else, the XML Name type, which looks like this:
[4] NameStartChar ::= ":" | [A-Z] | "_" | [a-z] | [#xC0-#xD6] | [#xD8-#xF6] | [#xF8-#x2FF] | [#x370-#x37D] | [#x37F-#x1FFF] | [#x200C-#x200D] | [#x2070-#x218F] | [#x2C00-#x2FEF] | [#x3001-#xD7FF] | [#xF900-#xFDCF] | [#xFDF0-#xFFFD] | [#x10000-#xEFFFF]

[4a] NameChar ::= NameStartChar | "-" | "." | [0-9] | #xB7 | [#x0300-#x036F] | [#x203F-#x2040]

[5] Name ::= NameStartChar (NameChar)*
Practically speaking, this means the allowed characters includes underscore, letters, digits, and also period "." and hyphen "-", but not colon.

I found the 2010 Biopax paper has a useful summary of what different parts of Biopax are about (p. 937)
The BioPAX community standard for pathway data sharing
Metabolic pathways are described using the ‘enzyme, substrate, product’ abstraction where substrates and products of a biochemical reaction are often small molecules. An enzyme, often a protein, catalyzes the reaction, and inhibitors and activators can modulate the catalysis event. Metabolic pathways use BioPAX classes: PhysicalEntity, Conversion, Catalysis, Modulation, Pathway.
Signaling pathways involve molecules and complexes participating in biochemical reactions, binding, transportation and catalysis events. These pathways may also include descriptions of molecular states (such as cellular location, covalent and noncovalent modifications, as well as fragments of sequence cleaved from a precursor) and generic molecules (such as the family of homologous Wnt proteins). Signaling pathways use BioPAX classes: PhysicalEntity, Conversion, Control, Catalysis, Modulation, MolecularInteraction, Pathway.
Gene regulatory networks involve transcription and translation events and their control. Transcription, translation and other template-directed reactions involving DNA or RNA are captured in a ‘template reaction’ in BioPAX, which maps a template to its encoded products (e.g., DNA to mRNA). Multiple sequence regions on a single strand of the template, such as promoters, terminators, open reading frames, operons and various reaction machinery bind- ing sites, are active in a template reaction. Transcription factors (generally proteins and complexes), microRNAs and other molecules, participate in a ‘template reaction regulation’ event. Gene regulatory networks use BioPAX classes: PhysicalEntity, TemplateReaction, TemplateReactionRegulation.
Molecular interactions, notably protein-protein protein-DNA interactions, involve two or more ‘physical entities’. BioPAX follows the standard representation scheme of the Proteomics Standards Initiative Molecular Interaction (PSI-MI) format. Molecular interactions use BioPAX classes: PhysicalEntity, MolecularInteraction.
Genetic interactions occur between two genes when the phenotypic consequence of perturbing both genes is different than expected given the phenotypes of each single gene perturbation. BioPAX represents this as a pair of genes that participate in a ‘genetic interaction’ measured using an observed ‘phenotype’. Genetic interactions use BioPAX classes: Gene, GeneticInteraction.

Summary from Chris Myers about how they convert logical/boolean models into SBML:
Hi Andreas,

I disagree. I’ve successfully encoded everything in SBML-qual using SBML-core. We use logical models in SBML all the time, but we do not use qual. The reason we haven’t used qual is because that it cannot interface with SBML-core, currently. L3V2 may fix this, which will make us reconsider, since sbml-qual does make the it a little more natural to express.

To see how this can be done, consider the simple logical model from the sbml-qual specification (Section 4.1):

http://co.mbine.org/specifications/sbml.level-3.version-1.qual.version-1.release-1.pdf

The way we do this in SBML-core is as follows:

Parameter A=0
Parameter B=0
Parameter C=0
Event A1
Trigger A<1 && C>=1
A:=1
Event A2
Trigger (A<=1 && A<2) || (C>=1 && A>=1)
A:=2
Event A0
Trigger !((A<1 && C>=1)&&((A<=1 && A<2) || (C>=1 && A>=1)))
A:=0
Event B1
Trigger A>=1
B:=1
Event B0
Trigger A<1
B:=0
Event C1
Trigger B>=1
C:=1
Event C0
Trigger B<1
C:=0

The result is a logical model completely semantically equivalent to the model in SBML-qual with the added advantage that it can be mixed with SBML-core constructs to produce a hybrid model (like the ones you describe in your paper). Granted qual does make it a little simpler to express, but with the downside that you cannot mix with core and use tools that support only SBML-core.

What might be interesting but perhaps out-of-scope this Summer, is seeing if L3V2 will enable us to leverage the more concise qual constructs to create a hybrid model like you describe. We could then implement a SBML-qual “flatten” routine that converts to core as I depict above enabling SBML-core tools to analyze such a model.
One more thing that I forgot to mention is that a similar translation can be done into species/reactions. Indeed, this is what we did in our state-based abstraction work (see Chapter 6 of my book and related publications). We actually first became interested in SBML due to the fact that it was powerful enough to allow us to express our models at all levels of abstraction. Namely, we were converting reaction-based models into state-based models in which species took logical values and were updated using logical equations. We were able to express our models throughout this abstraction process in SBML at all times.

Therefore, SBML-qual is a “syntactic” and NOT “semantic” extension of SBML. It allows you to express these types of models more concisely, but these types of models were always expressible in SBML.

One final thing, my concern is not about being “simulatable”. My concern is there exists no tools, to the best of my knowledge, that can open a hybrid model for any type of analysis or even visualization. This I think is a very significant concern.
Ok, just for fun, I reminded myself how this can be done with species/reactions (see attached model). Note that I simplified the logic a little bit from what is in the spec, but it is equivalent. Simulating this model reproduces the race condition. Namely, using gillespie simulation you can show that it can either move from state (1,0,0) to (0,0,0) and stay there OR from (1,0,0) to (2,1,1) and stay there. In other words, there are two stable states.

Again, I’m not arguing that qual is not useful and not a more simple, concise way to express this. I’m just trying to show that if we want to do core-only BioPax to SBML conversion that it is indeed possible.

Similarly, I’m also confident that we can do a qual-only BioPax to SBML conversion with a bit of thought.
